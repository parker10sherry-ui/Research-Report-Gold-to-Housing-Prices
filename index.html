Historical Gold Bull Run Cycle Analysis

1971–1980 Bull Run
511 oz (1971) → 109 oz (1980)

Gold went from $35 to $595/oz while homes rose from $25k to $65k. The ratio collapsed 79%, meaning gold massively outperformed housing. At the peak, you could nearly buy a house with just 109 ounces.

1980–2001 Bear Market
109 oz (1980) → 633 oz (2001)

Gold fell from $595 to $277 while homes rose from $65k to $175k. The ratio expanded 480%. Housing crushed gold for two decades. The 2001 peak was the worst reading in the entire series.

2001–2011 Bull Run
633 oz (2001) → 148 oz (2011)

Gold surged from $277 to $1,531 while homes went from $175k to $227k. The ratio fell 77%, nearly matching the 1970s compression. Gold again approached housing parity on an ounce basis.

2011–2026 Current Cycle
148 oz (2011) → ~80 oz (Now)

After expanding back to 280 oz (2015), gold's current parabolic run to $5,228 has compressed the ratio to ~80 oz. This is BELOW the 1980 trough of 109 oz, reaching territory not seen in the modern era.

The Pattern: Gold Bull Runs & Housing Affordability in Gold Terms

There is a clear cyclical pattern. During secular gold bull markets, the number of ounces required to purchase the median U.S. home compresses dramatically, typically falling 75–80% from peak to trough. In the 1970s, the ratio fell from ~510 oz to 109 oz. In the 2000s, it fell from ~633 oz to 148 oz.

The current cycle has now surpassed the 1980 extreme. At approximately 80 ounces today, gold's purchasing power relative to housing has reached levels never recorded in the post‑Bretton Woods era. Historically, these extremes have marked significant turning points: gold corrects, housing catches up, or both.

The reversal pattern is also consistent: After each gold bull run peak, the ratio expands for 15–20 years as gold mean‑reverts and housing appreciates. The 1980 trough took until 2001 to fully reverse. The question now is whether ~80 oz represents the cycle bottom, or if gold has further to run before exhaustion.
