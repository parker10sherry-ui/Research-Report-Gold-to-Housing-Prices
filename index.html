Gold Bull Run Cycle Analysis

1971–1980 Bull Run
616 oz (1971) → 96 oz (1980) needed to buy a home

Gold surged from $41 to $673/oz while homes rose from $25k to $65k. The ratio collapsed 84%. Two recessions (1973–75, 1980) coincided with gold's strongest legs higher. Inflation and monetary instability drove gold while housing grew modestly.

1980–2001 Bear Market
96 oz (1980) → 628 oz (2001) needed to buy a home

Gold fell from $673 to $275 while homes rose from $65k to $173k. The ratio expanded 550%. Disinflation, rising real rates, and a historic equity bull market starved gold of demand for two decades.

2001–2011 Bull Run
628 oz (2001) → 143 oz (2011) needed to buy a home

Gold surged from $275 to $1,572. The 2001 and 2008 recessions were launchpads for gold's strongest moves. The housing bust (2007–09) accelerated the ratio's compression as home prices fell while gold soared.

2011–2026 Current Cycle
143 oz (2011) → ~80 oz (Now, surpassing 1980 extreme)

After expanding to 253 oz (2015), gold's parabolic run to $5,228 has compressed the ratio below the 1980 trough of 96 oz. This is unprecedented in the post-Bretton Woods era.

Silver: The Volatile Cousin

Silver's Amplified Moves
Silver tends to move 1.5–3x gold's percentage moves

In the 1970s bull run, silver went from ~$1.55 to $20.80 (1,240% vs gold's 1,540%). In 2001–2011, silver rose from $4.38 to $35.22 (704% vs gold's 471%). Silver is the high-beta play on precious metals bull markets, but the drawdowns are equally vicious.

Gold:Silver Ratio as a Signal
Historical range: 15:1 to 125:1

When the gold:silver ratio exceeds 80:1, silver is historically cheap relative to gold. At the peaks of precious metals bull markets, the ratio compresses to 30–45:1. Today's ratio near 58:1 has compressed sharply from the 2020 extreme of 86:1, suggesting silver is finally catching up to gold — a classic late-cycle bull market signal.

Silver & Recessions
Industrial demand creates a different recession dynamic

Unlike gold, silver has ~50% industrial demand. It often drops initially during recessions (2008: silver fell from $20 to $9) as industrial demand falls, then rallies sharply in the recovery as monetary stimulus lifts all metals. The 2008–2011 move from $9 to $35 followed this exact pattern.

Silver/Housing Ratio
Currently ~12,900 oz of silver to buy a home

The silver-to-housing ratio shows even more extreme cyclicality than gold. At the 1980 peak, it took only ~3,111 oz. At the 2001 trough, it took ~39,400 oz. Today at ~4,677 oz, silver has gained massive ground vs housing and is approaching the 1980 extreme — suggesting silver's bull run is well underway.

The Pattern: Precious Metals, Housing & Recessions

Recessions are launchpads for precious metals. Every major gold bull run has been accelerated by recession. The 1973–75 recession saw gold surge from $95 to $169. The 2001 recession marked the exact bottom in gold at $275. The 2008 recession catalyzed gold's run from $872 to $1,572. The pattern is clear: monetary and fiscal responses to recession (rate cuts, QE, deficit spending) debase currencies and drive gold higher.

Silver lags, then leads. Silver typically underperforms gold early in a precious metals bull market and during recessions (due to industrial demand destruction), then dramatically outperforms in the latter stages. The gold:silver ratio acts as a compression spring — it widens during fear (86:1 in 2020), then snaps back violently during the speculative blow-off phase (currently compressing toward 58:1).

Housing is the slow anchor. Home prices rarely decline in nominal terms (2007–09 being the notable exception) and tend to rise steadily at 3–5% annually. This makes housing the denominator that moves slowly while gold and silver provide the volatile numerator. Ratios compress not because housing falls, but because metals surge.

The reversal warning: When gold can buy a house with historically few ounces (below ~150 oz), the precious metals bull market is typically mature. The 1980 extreme of 96 oz preceded a 20-year bear market in gold. Today at ~80 oz, we are deeper into "extreme territory" than any prior cycle.

Investment Timing: When to Buy & When to Sell

◆ BUY PRECIOUS METALS WHEN:
  • Gold:Housing ratio > 400 oz — Gold is historically cheap relative to real assets. The 2001 reading of 628 oz preceded a 5x gold rally.
  • Gold:Silver ratio > 80:1 — Silver is historically undervalued vs gold. This ratio exceeded 80:1 before every major silver bull run (86:1 in 2020 preceded silver's current surge).
  • Early recession / rate-cutting cycle — The first 6 months of a recession are the sweet spot. Gold rallied in every recession since 1970. Buy the fear, especially after the initial silver washout.
  • Negative real interest rates — When inflation exceeds the Fed Funds rate, gold thrives. This condition persisted through the 1970s and 2020–2023.
  • Silver/Housing ratio > 25,000 oz — Silver is dirt cheap versus real assets. The 2001 reading near 40,000 oz was the buy of a generation.
◆ SELL / REDUCE PRECIOUS METALS WHEN:
  • Gold:Housing ratio < 120 oz — Gold is historically expensive vs real assets. The 1980 reading of 96 oz preceded a 70% decline in gold over 20 years. Today's ~80 oz is even more extreme.
  • Gold:Silver ratio compresses to < 40:1 — Silver is catching up to gold, signaling speculative blow-off. Both 1980 (32:1) and 2011 (45:1) saw this before major tops. Currently at ~58:1 and compressing.
  • Parabolic price acceleration — When gold or silver doubles in < 12 months, the move is exhausting itself. Gold went from $3,333 avg in 2025 to $5,228 spot in early 2026. Silver hit an all-time high of $121.67 in Jan 2026.
  • Rising real interest rates — When the Fed raises rates above inflation, the opportunity cost of holding metals increases. Volcker's rate hikes in 1980–81 killed the gold bull. The 2013 taper tantrum knocked gold from $1,669 to $1,411.
  • Silver/Housing ratio < 3,000 oz — Silver is extremely expensive vs real assets. Approaching 1980-type mania territory.

Current Assessment: March 2026

Gold at $5,228/oz has surpassed the 1980 extreme in purchasing power terms. At ~80 oz to buy a median home, gold has never been this expensive relative to housing in the modern era. Historically, this signals a mature bull market — but one that could extend further before exhausting.

Silver at $89.81/oz has finally begun its catch-up move. The gold:silver ratio has compressed from 86:1 (2020) to ~58:1, which is approaching the historical average. Silver has surged 125% from its 2025 average of $39.80 and hit a nominal all-time high of $121.67 in January 2026. At ~4,677 oz of silver to buy a home, the silver:housing ratio is approaching but has not yet reached the 1980 extreme of ~3,111 oz.

The convergence is the signal. Both gold AND silver are now in "dear" territory versus housing. In past cycles, when the gold:silver ratio compresses below 40:1 during a metals bull market, it signals the final blow-off phase. At 58:1, we may be entering that window. The question is whether silver overshoots to 30:1 (implying ~$174/oz) or the cycle tops out here.

Risk factors: If a recession materializes, expect silver to drop 30–50% initially (industrial demand destruction) before rallying. Gold would likely hold up better during the downturn phase. However, silver's industrial demand profile has shifted — solar panel and EV demand now provide a structural floor that didn't exist in prior cycles.
