Gold Bull Run Cycle Analysis

1971–1980 Bull Run
511 oz → 109 oz needed to buy a home

Gold surged from $35 to $595/oz. The ratio collapsed 79%. Two recessions (1973–75, 1980) coincided with gold's strongest legs higher. Inflation and monetary instability drove gold while housing grew modestly.

1980–2001 Bear Market
109 oz → 633 oz needed to buy a home

Gold fell from $595 to $277 while homes tripled. The ratio expanded 480%. Disinflation, rising real rates, and a historic equity bull market starved gold of demand for two decades.

2001–2011 Bull Run
633 oz → 148 oz needed to buy a home

Gold surged from $277 to $1,531. The 2001 and 2008 recessions were launchpads for gold's strongest moves. The housing bust (2007–09) accelerated the ratio's compression as home prices fell while gold soared.

2011–2026 Current Cycle
148 oz → ~80 oz (surpassing 1980 extreme)

After expanding to 280 oz (2015), gold's parabolic run to $5,228 has compressed the ratio below the 1980 trough of 109 oz. This is unprecedented in the post-Bretton Woods era.

Silver: The Volatile Cousin

Silver's Amplified Moves
Silver tends to move 1.5–3x gold's percentage moves

In the 1970s bull run, silver went from ~$1.30 to $36 (2,700% vs gold's 1,600%). In 2001–2011, silver rose from $4.37 to $35 (700% vs gold's 450%). Silver is the high-beta play on precious metals bull markets, but the drawdowns are equally vicious.

Gold:Silver Ratio as a Signal
Historical range: 15:1 to 125:1

When the gold:silver ratio exceeds 80:1, silver is historically cheap relative to gold. At the peaks of precious metals bull markets, the ratio compresses to 30–40:1. Today's ratio near 160:1 is at historic extremes, suggesting silver has significant catch-up potential — or that structural demand has permanently shifted.

Silver & Recessions
Industrial demand creates a different recession dynamic

Unlike gold, silver has ~50% industrial demand. It often drops initially during recessions (2008: -57%) as industrial demand falls, then rallies sharply in the recovery as monetary stimulus lifts all metals. The 2008–2011 move from $9 to $49 followed this exact pattern.

Silver/Housing Ratio
Currently ~12,900 oz of silver to buy a home

The silver-to-housing ratio shows even more extreme cyclicality than gold. At the 1980 peak, it took only ~1,806 oz. At the 2001 trough, it took ~40,000 oz. Today at ~12,900 oz, silver has gained ground vs housing but remains far from the 1980 extreme, unlike gold which has surpassed it.

The Pattern: Precious Metals, Housing & Recessions

Recessions are launchpads for precious metals. Every major gold bull run has been accelerated by recession. The 1973–75 recession saw gold double. The 2001 recession marked the exact bottom in gold. The 2008 recession catalyzed gold's run from $870 to $1,900. The pattern is clear: monetary and fiscal responses to recession (rate cuts, QE, deficit spending) debase currencies and drive gold higher.

Silver lags, then leads. Silver typically underperforms gold early in a precious metals bull market and during recessions (due to industrial demand destruction), then dramatically outperforms in the latter stages. The gold:silver ratio acts as a compression spring — it widens during fear, then snaps back violently during the speculative blow-off phase.

Housing is the slow anchor. Home prices rarely decline in nominal terms (2007–09 being the notable exception) and tend to rise steadily at 3–5% annually. This makes housing the denominator that moves slowly while gold and silver provide the volatile numerator. Ratios compress not because housing falls, but because metals surge.

The reversal warning: When gold can buy a house with historically few ounces (below ~150 oz), the precious metals bull market is typically mature. The 1980 extreme of 109 oz preceded a 20-year bear market in gold. Today at ~80 oz, we are deeper into "extreme territory" than any prior cycle.

Investment Timing: When to Buy & When to Sell

◆ BUY PRECIOUS METALS WHEN:
  • Gold:Housing ratio > 400 oz — Gold is historically cheap relative to real assets. The 2001 reading of 633 oz preceded a 5x gold rally.
  • Gold:Silver ratio > 80:1 — Silver is historically undervalued vs gold. This ratio exceeded 80:1 before every major silver bull run. Today's ~160:1 is at an all-time extreme.
  • Early recession / rate-cutting cycle — The first 6 months of a recession are the sweet spot. Gold rallied in every recession since 1970. Buy the fear, especially after the initial silver washout.
  • Negative real interest rates — When inflation exceeds the Fed Funds rate, gold thrives. This condition persisted through the 1970s and 2020–2023.
  • Silver/Housing ratio > 25,000 oz — Silver is dirt cheap versus real assets. The 2001 reading near 40,000 oz was the buy of a generation.
◆ SELL / REDUCE PRECIOUS METALS WHEN:
  • Gold:Housing ratio < 120 oz — Gold is historically expensive vs real assets. The 1980 reading of 109 oz preceded a 70% decline in gold over 20 years. Today's ~80 oz is even more extreme.
  • Gold:Silver ratio compresses to < 40:1 — Silver is catching up to gold, signaling speculative blow-off. Both 1980 (16:1) and 2011 (32:1) saw this before major tops.
  • Parabolic price acceleration — When gold or silver doubles in < 12 months, the move is exhausting itself. Gold went from $400 to $850 in the final 2 months of 1979. Silver went from $18 to $49 in 5 months in 2011.
  • Rising real interest rates — When the Fed raises rates above inflation, the opportunity cost of holding metals increases. Volcker's rate hikes in 1980–81 killed the gold bull. The 2013 taper tantrum knocked gold from $1,700 to $1,200.
  • Silver/Housing ratio < 3,000 oz — Silver is extremely expensive vs real assets. Approaching 1980-type mania territory.

Current Assessment: March 2026

Gold at $5,228/oz has surpassed the 1980 extreme in purchasing power terms. At ~80 oz to buy a median home, gold has never been this expensive relative to housing in the modern era. Historically, this signals a mature bull market — but one that could extend further before exhausting.

Silver at $32.60/oz tells a very different story. The gold:silver ratio near 160:1 is at all-time highs, far exceeding the prior extreme of 125:1 in 2020. This suggests either: (a) silver has massive catch-up potential and could 3–5x from here, or (b) the traditional relationship between gold and silver has structurally broken.

The divergence is the signal. In past cycles, gold led and silver eventually followed with even larger percentage gains. If history rhymes, silver's move is still ahead. But if the gold:housing ratio is screaming "sell gold," and the gold:silver ratio is screaming "buy silver," the nuanced play may be rotating from gold into silver — selling the asset that has already achieved an extreme, into the one that hasn't yet.

Risk factors: If a recession materializes, expect silver to drop 30–50% initially (industrial demand destruction) before rallying. Gold would likely hold up better during the downturn phase. The optimal entry for silver would be during the recession washout, not before it.
